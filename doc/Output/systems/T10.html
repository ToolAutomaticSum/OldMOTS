[0] Born in 1541, in either the village of Fodele or Candia (the Venetian name of Chandax, present day Heraklion) on Crete, El Greco was descended from a prosperous urban family, which had probably been driven out of Chania to Candia after an uprising against the Venetians between 1526 and 1528. [1] El Greco received his initial training as an icon painter of the Cretan school, the leading centre of post-Byzantine art. [2] Unlike other Cretan artists who had moved to Venice, El Greco substantially altered his style and sought to distinguish himself by inventing new and unusual interpretations of traditional religious subject matter. [3] By the time El Greco arrived in Rome, Michelangelo and Raphael were dead, but their example continued to be paramount, and somewhat overwhelming for young painters. [4] Mature works and later years [5] Art historian Max Dvořák was the first scholar to connect El Greco's art with Mannerism and Antinaturalism. [6] In the marginalia that El Greco inscribed in his copy of Daniele Barbaro's translation of Vitruvius' De architectura, he refuted Vitruvius' attachment to archaeological remains, canonical proportions, perspective and mathematics. [7] —Nikos Kazantzakis, Report to Greco [8] —Julius Meier-Graefe, The Spanish Journey [9] The early cubist explorations of Picasso were to uncover other aspects in the work of El Greco: structural analysis of his compositions, multi-faced refraction of form, interweaving of form and space, and special effects of highlights. [10] In 1998, the Greek electronic composer and artist Vangelis published El Greco, a symphonic album inspired by the artist.
